\section{Introduction}

Understanding the determinants of household electricity consumption is central to energy policy, utility demand forecasting, and residential carbon mitigation. As the electrification of heating, transportation, and cooking accelerates, quantifying the structural and behavioural drivers of residential electricity use becomes increasingly important for grid planning and rate design.

This report presents an end-to-end regression analysis of annual household electricity consumption (KWH) using the 2020 Residential Energy Consumption Survey (RECS) microdata published by the U.S.\ Energy Information Administration (EIA). RECS is the most comprehensive nationally representative survey of U.S.\ residential energy use, covering dwelling characteristics, appliance inventories, occupant demographics, thermostat behaviour, climate zones, and metered energy consumption outcomes.

The analysis proceeds through five stages: (1)~data inspection and cleaning, (2)~exploratory data analysis, (3)~multicollinearity diagnostics and feature selection, (4)~OLS model specification with robust inference, and (5)~benchmarking against regularised and tree-based models.

Three specific goals guide the work:

\begin{itemize}
\item Pin down the dominant household-level drivers of electricity use through an interpretable OLS model with heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors.
\item Compare predictive performance across OLS, Ridge, Lasso, Elastic Net, PCA-based regression, and HistGradientBoosting on both log and raw KWH scales.
\item Test whether engineered composite features (like an electronics load proxy or space-per-person) add real explanatory power on top of the raw survey fields.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Cleaning and Feature Engineering}

RECS encodes missing or inapplicable responses using special codes: $-2$ for not applicable, $-1$ for missing, 97--99 for refused or don't know. The thermostat fields (\texttt{TEMPHOME}, \texttt{TEMPGONE}, \texttt{TEMPNITE} and their summer counterparts) use a different convention: 997, 998, and 999. We replaced all of these with NaN before doing anything else.

Categorical codes got mapped to readable labels (\texttt{TYPEHUQ} $1 \rightarrow$ ``Mobile home'', $2 \rightarrow$ ``Single-family detached'', and so on). We also created six engineered features:

\begin{itemize}
\item \texttt{log\_conditioned\_sqft}: $\log(1 + \text{sqft})$, which linearises the size-consumption curve.
\item \texttt{sqft\_per\_person}: conditioned area divided by household size, a density proxy.
\item \texttt{ac\_unit\_count}: total of ductless, window/wall, and portable AC units.
\item \texttt{electronics\_load\_proxy}: count of TVs, streaming devices, consoles, audio systems, desktops, laptops, tablets, and smartphones.
\item \texttt{children\_share}: fraction of household members who are children.
\item \texttt{summer\_setpoint\_spread}: thermostat away temperature minus home daytime temperature, capturing setback discipline.
\end{itemize}

After all cleaning and engineering, the working dataframe has 18{,}496 rows and 99 columns. We split it 80/20 (14{,}796 train, 3{,}700 test), stratified by census region so geographic coverage stays balanced.

\section{Exploratory Data Analysis}

\subsection{Climate and Housing Type}

Hot-Humid climates, concentrated in the U.S.\ Southeast, top the chart at about 13{,}000 median KWH, roughly double the Subarctic median of 6{,}500 (Figure~\ref{fig:climate_housing}). That gap is mostly cooling load. On the housing side, mobile homes consume the most (median around 11{,}000~KWH), probably because of poor insulation and minimal shared-wall area. Apartment 5+ units sit at the bottom near 5{,}000~KWH, reflecting smaller footprints and shared walls.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Modelling Framework}

The workflow has four phases: OLS baseline with robust inference, multicollinearity diagnostics and pruning, regularised linear models, and gradient-boosted regression. Every model predicts $y = \log(1 + \text{KWH})$ on the same 80/20 split, stratified by census region. We report results on both the log scale (where fitting happens) and the original KWH scale (via $\hat{y}_{\text{KWH}} = e^{\hat{y}_{\log}} - 1$). Jensen's inequality introduces a small negative bias in the back-transformation, which we accept since our focus is on relative model comparison.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

Every model gets five metrics on both scales. $R^2$ measures variance explained (train and test; adjusted $R^2$ on train only, since it has no clear meaning on held-out data). RMSE penalises large errors. MAE gives a robust central-tendency measure. MAPE is the most business-friendly: ``the model is off by X\% of actual consumption on average.''

\subsection{Preprocessing}

Numeric features are median-imputed (robust to skew). Binary features get most-frequent imputation. Categoricals are most-frequent imputed and then one-hot encoded, dropping the first level to avoid collinearity. Regularised models additionally standardise numerics to zero mean and unit variance. OLS keeps raw units so the coefficients are directly interpretable.

\subsection{Baseline}

A naive mean baseline (predict $\bar{y}_{\text{train}}$ for every observation) sets the floor. Its test $R^2$ is roughly zero on log scale and actually negative on KWH scale, because back-transforming a log-scale mean prediction undershoots. Any real model needs to beat this by a wide margin.

\section{Multicollinearity Diagnostics}

Before specifying OLS, we screened 16 numeric candidates using variance inflation factors. The initial set had severe problems (Table~\ref{tab:vif}): \texttt{log\_conditioned\_sqft} came in at VIF~=~127, \texttt{conditioned\_sqft} at 82, \texttt{heated\_sqft} at 67. Not surprising, since total area, heated area, and cooled area are nearly the same number for most homes.

We kept one representative per collinear cluster. For dwelling size, \texttt{log\_conditioned\_sqft} stayed while all the raw area variants, room counts, and heated/cooled shares were dropped. For layout, \texttt{full\_bathrooms} was enough (bedrooms and total\_rooms both correlate above 0.6 with sqft). For occupancy, we kept both \texttt{household\_size} and \texttt{num\_children} because they carry distinct signals: size captures total load while children captures a differential per-capita effect. For climate, \texttt{cooling\_degree\_days} stayed and heating degree days got dropped since the two are highly collinear once the other size variables are gone.

\subsection{Initial vs.\ Pruned OLS}

The initial OLS on the candidate set (33 regressors after one-hot encoding) gave train/test $R^2$ of 0.708/0.703 on the log scale. After checking HC3 robust $p$-values, we dropped four groups. \texttt{urbanicity\_band} added little beyond what housing type and climate already captured. \texttt{employment\_band} was weakly significant and conceptually mediated through dwelling size and appliance ownership anyway. \texttt{uses\_propane} affected fewer than 5\% of households with a small, unstable coefficient. And \texttt{householder\_age} was only marginally significant ($t = -2.0$, $p = 0.043$), with less direct explanatory power than the occupancy variables already in the model.

The pruned model uses 25 regressors and barely loses anything: 0.705/0.699. AIC goes from 11{,}840 to 11{,}980, and BIC actually favours the simpler model thanks to the complexity penalty. Good trade.

\subsection{Pruned OLS Coefficients (HC3)}

All inference uses HC3 heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors, which stay consistent under heteroskedasticity without needing to specify a particular variance structure. Table~\ref{tab:ols} has the full coefficient table.

Every numeric and binary predictor is significant at $p < 0.001$. Among the climate dummies (Cold is the reference), Mixed-Humid ($t = 21.2$), Very-Cold ($t = 6.7$), and Marine ($t = 5.3$) are clearly significant. Hot-Humid is not individually significant ($p = 0.114$), though the climate group as a whole is. For housing type (Apartment 2--4 is reference), Mobile home has the biggest positive effect ($\beta = 0.399$, $t = 18.5$) and Apartment 5+ goes negative ($\beta = -0.142$, $t = -8.6$).

\subsection{Residual Diagnostics}

The residuals-vs-fitted plot (Figure~\ref{fig:resid}, left) looks roughly symmetric with no obvious curve, though variance does tick up a bit at higher fitted values. The QQ plot (right) peels away from the reference line in the left tail, consistent with the $-0.44$ skew of the log target.

Formal tests back this up (Table~\ref{tab:diag}). Jarque-Bera is 7{,}296 ($p \approx 0$; kurtosis 6.23 versus the normal value of 3.0). Breusch-Pagan rejects homoskedasticity with LM = 309 and $p < 10^{-50}$. Both of these are why we use HC3 throughout. Durbin-Watson comes in at 2.05, so no autocorrelation to worry about in this cross-sectional data.

\subsection{Analysis of Results}

The linear models are essentially identical. Pruned OLS, sklearn's Linear Regression, Ridge, Lasso, and Elastic Net all land at test $R^2 \approx 0.699$ on log scale and about 0.59 on KWH. Regularisation gives zero meaningful lift, which tells us something important: careful manual feature selection through VIF diagnostics already did the job that automated shrinkage would do. There is no overfitting left to penalise.

The engineered feature set bumps linear models from 0.699 to 0.710 (+1.1~pp on test). This gain is consistent across all four linear families, suggesting that \texttt{electronics\_load\_proxy} and \texttt{ac\_unit\_count} capture real device-load effects that individual appliance counts miss.

HistGradientBoosting is where things get interesting. It reaches test $R^2 = 0.761$ on the expanded set and 0.771 on the engineered set, a 6--7~pp jump over any linear model. On the KWH scale that becomes 0.714--0.719 versus roughly 0.59. The train-test gap for Engineered HGB is 0.803 vs.\ 0.771 (3.2~pp), which is moderate and acceptable. The performance gap points to interaction effects (climate $\times$ AC status, dwelling size $\times$ occupancy) and nonlinear thresholds that additive models just cannot represent. The marginal value of another AC unit, for example, almost certainly depends on whether you live in Houston or Seattle; HGB captures this through its tree splits automatically.

PCA + Linear does worse than plain OLS by about 1~pp. Compressing the numerics into principal components washes out axis-aligned signals that the individual features carry.

One more pattern worth calling out: $R^2$ drops substantially when we convert from log to KWH scale (Pruned OLS goes from 0.699 to 0.590). That is expected. The $\text{expm1}(\cdot)$ back-transformation amplifies errors for high-consumption households, and Jensen's inequality means $E[\exp(\hat{y})] \neq \exp(E[\hat{y}])$, so there is a systematic negative bias. A Duan smearing estimator would fix this, but we did not apply it since we care about relative model ranking, not point forecasts.

The naive baseline gives test $R^2 = -0.001$ on log and $-0.071$ on KWH. The negative KWH number confirms that predicting the log-mean and exponentiating actually does worse than just predicting the raw-mean directly. Every fitted model blows past this floor.

\subsection{Cross-Model Feature Importance}

The feature ranking barely moves across Pruned OLS, Linear Regression, Ridge, and Lasso (Figure~\ref{fig:grouped}). \texttt{housing\_unit\_type} and \texttt{ba\_climate} dominate in all four. Then come \texttt{has\_air\_conditioning}, \texttt{all\_electric\_home}, and \texttt{has\_dryer}. That kind of stability across very different estimation methods is a good sign: the core feature set is robust, not an artefact of any one model's assumptions.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Coefficient Interpretation}

With $\log(1 + \text{KWH})$ as the target, each coefficient approximates a percentage change in KWH per unit increase. For coefficients below about 0.2 the approximation is decent; larger ones need exponentiation.

A 1\% increase in conditioned sqft maps to roughly 0.24\% higher annual KWH (log-log elasticity). Air conditioning adds about 33\% ($e^{0.284} - 1$). All-electric homes use about 30\% more ($e^{0.263} - 1$), while having natural gas access cuts electricity by roughly 20\% ($1 - e^{-0.227}$), a clean fuel-substitution effect. Each additional household member adds around 14\%, though children contribute less: net $+$11\% per child versus $+$14\% per adult. And mobile homes consume about 49\% more ($e^{0.399} - 1$) than the Apartment 2--4 reference, most likely because of poor insulation.

\subsection{Limitations}

Heteroskedasticity persists despite the log transform (Breusch-Pagan $p < 10^{-50}$). HC3 fixes inference but does nothing for prediction intervals. Residual non-normality is also an issue: kurtosis of 6.23 means normality-based prediction bands would be too narrow, though coefficient $p$-values remain asymptotically valid at $n = 14{,}796$.

Our estimates are unweighted. RECS has a complex survey design, and population-level inference technically requires incorporating the survey weights and strata. The coefficients we report are sample-conditional relationships, which may differ from population-average effects.

We also rely on a single 80/20 split. Cross-validation was used inside Ridge, Lasso, and Elastic Net for tuning, but the final $R^2$ comparisons across models are single-realisation point estimates with no variance attached.

Finally, several plausibly important variables are missing from the analysis: local electricity prices, building insulation quality, HVAC system efficiency ratings, and more granular behavioural data beyond thermostat settings.

\subsection{Practical Implications}

The OLS model points directly to the highest-consumption segment: mobile homes in hot-humid climates. Weatherisation and insulation retrofit programmes aimed here would get the most savings per household.

The 30\% electricity premium for all-electric homes (after controlling for everything else) quantifies the grid impact of fuel switching. As gas heating gets phased out, utilities should expect a proportional jump in winter peak demand.

Appliance counts matter more than people might expect. Each additional freezer adds roughly 15\% to annual consumption. \texttt{electronics\_load\_proxy} coming in significant suggests appliance efficiency standards and electronics power management still have teeth as policy levers.

And the thermostat results are sobering for behavioural-intervention advocates. The weak univariate signal from setpoints, combined with structural features dominating the OLS, suggests that nudge-based programmes (smart thermostats, energy reports) will produce smaller savings than structural investments like insulation upgrades, HVAC replacements, and tighter building codes.

\subsection{Model Selection Guidance}

For interpretability (regulatory filings, policy briefs), the pruned OLS with HC3 is the right choice. Coefficients are directly readable, $p$-values come with robust standard errors, and the model is completely transparent.

For prediction (demand forecasting, customer segmentation), HistGradientBoosting on the engineered set is the winner, with test $R^2 = 0.77$ on log scale and MAPE around 26\% on KWH. That is a solid improvement over the linear models' 30\%.

\section{Conclusion}

A compact set of dwelling, equipment, occupancy, and climate features explains about 70\% of the log-variance in U.S.\ household electricity consumption. The pruned OLS (test $R^2 = 0.699$) with HC3 robust SEs identifies housing type, climate zone, air conditioning, all-electric status, dwelling size, and dryer ownership as the main drivers.

Regularised linear models land in the same spot as OLS, confirming the specification is already near-efficient for linear methods. HistGradientBoosting pushes test $R^2$ to 0.77 on log scale (0.72 on KWH), picking up nonlinearities and interactions that linear models miss. Engineered features add about 1~pp to linear models and give HGB its best result, validating that composites like \texttt{electronics\_load\_proxy} and \texttt{sqft\_per\_person} carry real incremental signal.

The bottom line: household electricity consumption is mostly a structural and geographic story. The type and size of the dwelling, the climate it sits in, and whether it runs on electricity alone explain most of the variance. Behavioural factors (thermostat habits, lighting, appliance usage) are secondary.

\subsection*{Future Work}

A few natural extensions come to mind. Incorporating survey weights through weighted least squares would make the coefficient estimates population-representative. Adding interaction terms (climate $\times$ AC, housing type $\times$ all-electric) could close part of the gap between OLS and HGB without giving up interpretability. State-level electricity price data from EIA could be merged in to test price elasticity. And since the 2020 survey coincides with COVID-19 stay-at-home orders, comparing against a future RECS wave would show whether the at-home effect on consumption was temporary or permanent.
